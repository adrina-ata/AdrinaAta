# When Cells Do Not Forget Pressure: A New Approach in Cancer Therapy

## Introduction

For many years, cancer has been primarily considered a genetic disease. However, recent studies have revealed that physical forces from the cellular microenvironment also play a crucial role in cancer progression. In many tumors, the surrounding extracellular matrix becomes stiffer than normal tissue, sending signals to cancer cells that promote their growth, survival, and invasion. Scientists are now investigating how cells sense these mechanical forces and convert them into biological signals.

Cellular Mechanical Memory

Recent discoveries have shown that cells can temporarily remember mechanical forces from their environment. When a short-term mechanical stress is applied to a cell, it is often ignored or rapidly reversed. However, when this force persists for a longer period, cells respond by forming stable fibrillar adhesions and connecting them to the vimentin cytoskeleton.

During this process, proteins such as plectin play an important role in establishing communication between the cytoskeleton and the nucleus. These structures allow the nucleus to maintain changes in its shape even after the mechanical stress is removed. Research has demonstrated that this mechanical response can persist for approximately one hour after the removal of the external force. This phenomenon is known as cellular mechanical memory.

Potential Applications in Cancer Treatment

The discovery of cellular mechanical memory may have significant implications for the development of new cancer therapies. Researchers suggest that disrupting the transmission of mechanical signals could potentially reduce cancer cell growth and invasion.

Therefore, scientists are exploring biotechnology-based approaches and targeted drug development strategies to interfere with proteins involved in cellular mechanical memory. By targeting these pathways, future therapies may be able to influence how cancer cells respond to their physical environment.

Conclusion

The discovery of cellular mechanical memory demonstrates that cancer cells are influenced not only by genetic alterations but also by the physical forces surrounding them. Targeting the mechanisms involved in mechanical memory could represent a novel strategy for cancer treatment and open new possibilities in the design of next-generation anticancer therapies.

Future drugs may not only target genetic mutations but also regulate how cells sense, interpret, and remember mechanical signals from their environment.
